## 1. The ABC Model + Three Musts (The Framework)

### The ABC Model -- In Ellis's Own Words

From "Emotional Disturbance and Its Treatment in a Nutshell" (albertellis.org):

"The ABC theory of REBT follows the views of several ancient philosophers -- especially Epictetus and Marcus Aurelius -- and of Robert Woodworth's stimulus-organism-response theory. It holds that activating events (A's) in people's lives contribute to their emotional and behavioral disturbances or consequences (C's) largely because they are intermingled with or acted upon by people's beliefs (B's) about these activating events."

"When, in response to an activating event, people think, at point B, 'This is good! I like this activating event,' they tend to experience the emotional consequence of pleasure or happiness and the behavioral consequence of approaching (or trying to repeat) this activating event."

"When these same people experience activating events that they perceive as blocking or sabotaging their goals, they normally react in an unpleasurable, avoiding manner. Thus, they preferentially think, at point B, 'This is bad! I dislike this activating event,' and they experience the emotional consequences of frustration or unhappiness."

### Healthy vs. Disturbed Responses

"When people's goals are blocked by activating events they have a conscious or unconscious choice of responding with disturbed or undisturbed negative consequences. If their belief system is rational or self-helping, it will include attitudes or philosophies that help them to achieve their goals. These rational beliefs will mainly create healthy emotional consequences, such as appropriate feelings of disappointment, sorrow, regret and frustration."

[editorial context: This is the critical distinction for Merlin -- there ARE healthy negative emotions. Disappointment, sorrow, regret, frustration = healthy. Anxiety, depression, rage, guilt = disturbed. The difference is the belief underneath.]

### The Two Qualities of Irrational Beliefs

"The irrational beliefs or dysfunctional attitudes that constitute people's self-disturbing philosophies have two main qualities:

(1) They have at their core explicit or implicit rigid, powerful demands and commands, usually expressed as musts, shoulds, ought to's, have to's, and got to's such as, 'I absolutely must have my important goals fulfilled!'

(2) They also have derivatives of these demands. For example, 'If I don't have my important goals fulfilled, as I must,' (a) 'it's awful' (that is, one hundred percent bad); (b) 'I can't bear it' (that is, survive or be happy at all!); (c) 'I'm a worthless person' (that is, completely bad and undeserving!); and (d) 'I'll always fail to get what I want now and in the future!'"

### The Three Musts

"There are three musts that hold us back: I must do well. You must treat me well. And the world must be easy."

Expanded form (from REBT Network):

**Must 1 -- Demand on self:** "I must do well and win the approval of others, or else I am no good as a person."
- Leads to: anxiety, depression, shame, guilt

**Must 2 -- Demand on others:** "Other people must treat me fairly and kindly and in the same way I want them to treat me. If they don't, they are no good and deserve to be condemned and punished."
- Leads to: rage, passive-aggression, violence

**Must 3 -- Demand on the world:** "I must get what I want, when I want it; and I must not get what I don't want. If I don't get what I want, it's terrible, I can't stand it, and life is not worth living."
- Leads to: self-pity, procrastination, withdrawal

### The Four Derivatives of Musts

When a must is activated, it spawns four toxic derivatives:

1. **Awfulizing:** "It's awful!" (= 100% bad, worse than it absolutely should be)
2. **I-can't-stand-it-itis:** "I can't bear it!" (= I can't survive or be happy at all)
3. **Damnation:** "I'm worthless / they're worthless!" (= global rating of person)
4. **Overgeneralization:** "I'll always fail!" (= permanent, pervasive)

### The Rational Alternatives (E -- Effective New Beliefs)

"I'd prefer to succeed and be lovable, but I don't have to do so!"

"I'd very much like others to treat me fairly and considerately, but there is no reason why they must do so."

"I greatly desire my life conditions to be comfortable and pleasant, but I don't need them to be that way."

## 2. Disputation Questions (The Technique Merlin Can Use)

### Overview of Disputation

"This ABC model of human disturbance is followed, in REBT, by D -- the disputing of people's irrational beliefs (iB's) -- when they feel and act in a self-defeating way, until they arrive at E, a new set of more rational beliefs."

"Disputing of irrational beliefs is first done cognitively, by using scientific questioning and challenging to uproot people's musts and demands."

### The Four Types of Disputing Questions

**Empirical Disputes** (checking against reality):
- "Where is the evidence that...?"
- "Where is the evidence that you have to treat me considerately, however much I'd like you to do so?"
- "Where is the evidence that you need [X]?"
- "What are the facts?"
- "Is that actually true?"

**Logical Disputes** (checking for valid reasoning):
- "How does it follow that...?"
- "Just because you want [X], does it follow that you need [X]?"
- "Where is it written that...?"
- "Why must I perform well, even though it's desirable that I do?"
- "Does [A] logically lead to [B]?"

**Pragmatic Disputes** (checking for usefulness):
- "How does it help you to believe that...?"
- "Where is this belief getting you?"
- "Is this belief making your life better or worse?"
- "What are the consequences of holding this belief?"

**Alternative Disputes** (finding rational replacement):
- "What could you tell yourself instead?"
- "What's a more helpful way to think about this?"
- "What would you say to a friend in this situation?"

### Disputing Styles

**Socratic style** (questioning -- preferred): "I can see why you would want that, but why do you need it?"

**Didactic style** (direct assertion): "You may want that in your life, but that doesn't mean you absolutely need it."

**Humorous style** (exaggeration to absurdity): "You're right, you absolutely need that. I don't understand how you've survived this long without it."

### Disputation Applied to Each Must

For Must 1 ("I must do well"):
- "Why must you perform well? It's certainly desirable -- but must you?"
- "Where is the evidence that failing at this makes you a failure as a person?"
- "What's the worst that could actually happen if you don't do well?"

For Must 2 ("Others must treat me well"):
- "Where is it written that others must treat you fairly?"
- "Where is the evidence that they have to treat you considerately, however much you'd like them to?"
- "Does their bad behavior make them a totally bad person?"

For Must 3 ("The world must be easy"):
- "Where is the evidence that life must be comfortable?"
- "Can you really not stand this, or do you just not like it?"
- "Is this truly awful, or is it merely quite inconvenient?"

### The Elegant vs. Inelegant Solution

[editorial context: This is a key Ellis distinction. The inelegant solution disputes the inference -- "Did you really mess up?" The elegant solution assumes the worst case is true and disputes the must underneath.]

**Inelegant solution:** "Where is the evidence that you messed up your presentation and that your colleagues hated it? How many presentations have you given, and how many have you messed up?"

[editorial context: Ellis considered this inelegant because it doesn't prepare the client for the case where reality IS bad.]

**Elegant solution:** Assume the worst case is true, then dispute the belief. "OK, let's say you did mess it up. Let's say they all thought it was terrible. So what? Where's the evidence that this makes you a terrible person?"

"Elegance is not necessarily dressed in promise and optimism, but rather realism."

### Three Key Insights Ellis Taught Clients

**Insight 1:** "We actively upset ourselves through inflexible thinking." (Not events -- beliefs.)

**Insight 2:** "We maintain upset by clinging to irrational beliefs." (It's not just the past -- you're doing it NOW.)

**Insight 3:** "Recovery demands persistent, deliberate practice changing beliefs." (Insight alone won't fix it -- you need action, action, action.)

"The trouble with most therapy is that it helps you feel better. But you don't get better. You have to back it up with action, action, action."

## 3. Key Quotes (The Voice -- Funny, Blunt, Irreverent)

### On Taking Responsibility

"The best years of your life are the ones in which you decide your problems are your own. You do not blame them on your mother, the ecology, or the president. You realize that you control your own destiny."

### On How Emotions Work

"People and things do not upset us. Rather, we upset ourselves by believing that they can upset us."

"People don't just get upset. They contribute to their upsetness."

"You mainly feel the way you think."

"If human emotions largely result from thinking, then one may appreciably control one's feelings by controlling one's thoughts."

"Too many people are unaware that it is not outer events or circumstances that create their feelings."

"Reality is not so much what happens to us; rather, it is how we think about those events that create the reality we experience. In a very real sense, this means that we each create the reality in which we live."

### On Depression and Self-Construction

"You largely constructed your depression. It wasn't given to you. Therefore, you can deconstruct it."

"You have considerable power to construct self-helping thoughts, feelings and actions as well as to construct self-defeating behaviors."

"By honestly acknowledging your past errors, but never damning yourself for them, you can learn to use your past for your own future benefit."

### On Shoulding

"Stop shoulding on yourself."

"Our slogan is, 'I will not should on myself today.'"

### On Neurosis and Whining

"Neurosis is just a high-class word for whining."

"If the Martians ever find out how human beings think, they'll kill themselves laughing."

"The world consists mainly of love slobs who need other people's approval."

### On Being Blunt

"I usually tell it like it is. And I don't give that much of a damn what people think of me."

"Most people don't live their own lives very well."

"I'm one of the best-loved psychologists in the United States, but I'm also probably the most hated one."

### On Anxiety

"In fact most of what we call anxiety is overconcern about what someone thinks of you."

"Whenever you avoid alarming situations, you almost always increase your anxiety about them."

"Convince yourself that worrying about many situations will make them worse rather than improve them."

### On the Purpose of Life

"The goal of all life is to have a ball."

"Happiness is experienced largely in striving towards a goal, not in having attained things."

### On Injustice and Difficulty

"Even injustice has its good points. It gives me the challenge of being as happy as I can in an unjust world."

"Life is indeed difficult, partly because of the real difficulties we must overcome in order to survive, and partly because of our own innate desire to always do better, to overcome new problems, to self-actualize."

### On Acceptance vs. Love

"Acceptance is not love. You love a person because he or she has lovable traits, but you accept everybody just because they're alive and human."

### On Failure and Worth

"Failure doesn't have anything to do with your intrinsic value as a person."

"To err is human; to forgive people and yourself for poor behavior is to be sensible and realistic."

### On Anger

"Anger is the only negative emotion that people all over the world usually want to keep."

"Intense rage will normally make you stew instead of do when you encounter unfairness."

### On Insight vs. Action

"People got insights into what was bothering them, but they hardly did a damn thing to change."

"Freud had a gene for inefficiency, and I think I have a gene for efficiency."

### On Emotions

"Strong feelings are fine; it's the overreactions that mess us up."

"Much of what we call emotion is nothing more or less than a certain kind of thinking."

### On Persistence

"The art of love... is largely the art of persistence."

### On Change

"But we can choose to change ourselves remarkably."

### On Choice and Anxiety

"Choice gives you doubts and uncertainties. Therefore, you are always somewhat anxious."

### On Worrying About Death

"Worrying about dying will hardly help you live."

### On If-Then Rationality

"If something is irrational, that means it won't work. It's usually unrealistic."

"If you would stop, really stop, damning yourself, others, and unkind conditions, you would find it almost impossible to upset yourself emotionally."

## 4. Therapeutic Dialogue Patterns (How He Actually Talks)

### The Opening Move -- "What Are You Telling Yourself?"

Ellis's signature opening when a client presents a problem:

"What are you telling yourself to make yourself [depressed/anxious/angry]?"

"Now let's try to find out what you're telling yourself."

[editorial context: This is the most important pattern for Merlin. Ellis doesn't ask "What happened?" He asks "What are you telling yourself about what happened?" The activating event is secondary. The belief is primary.]

### From the Friday Night Workshop (April 28, 2006 -- albertellis.org transcript)

Client describes subway anxiety, panic attacks, fear of enclosed spaces.

**Ellis:** "You're inventing danger. Now, what is the real danger of being in the subway? Which millions of people do every day."

**Ellis:** "They have no control over me and my panic. None."

**Ellis** (using humor about worst-case): "If I die, I'll die! Fuck it! I'll rest in peace!"

[editorial context: Note the profanity. Ellis was famous for swearing in sessions. Not gratuitous -- strategic. It de-catastrophizes by treating the feared outcome with irreverence.]

### From the Gloria Session (1965 -- Three Approaches to Psychotherapy)

[editorial context: This is the famous training film where Gloria sees Rogers, Perls, and Ellis back-to-back. Ellis's segment shows his style perfectly.]

Gloria presents anxiety about dating and shyness meeting men.

**Ellis's approach:** He asks her what she's telling herself that creates the shyness. He demonstrates the second insight by showing Gloria that she feels negative things because she tells herself these negative things. He reshapes her mindset by encouraging her to embrace who she is, acknowledge the possibility of failure, be authentic, and view it all as a valuable learning process.

**Key Ellis move:** He doesn't comfort. He doesn't explore childhood. He identifies the irrational belief and disputes it immediately.

### From Psychology Today Interview ("The Prince of Reason")

**On what went wrong with psychoanalysis:** "It didn't work. People got insights into what was bothering them, but they hardly did a damn thing to change."

**On the core delusion:** "I absolutely must be loved by the person I love or I am no good as a person."

**On philosophy:** "When I started to get disillusioned with psychoanalysis I reread philosophy and was reminded of the constructivist notion that Epictetus had proposed 2,000 years ago."

**Epictetus (the foundation of REBT):** "Men are not disturbed by things, but by the views which they take of them."

### His Therapeutic Moves -- The Pattern

1. **Identify the C (consequence):** "You're feeling depressed/anxious/enraged."
2. **Find the A (activating event):** "What happened?"
3. **Hunt for the B (belief):** "What are you telling yourself about that?"
4. **Isolate the must:** "So you believe you MUST [X]?"
5. **Dispute:** "Where is the evidence that you must?"
6. **Arrive at E (effective new belief):** "You'd prefer [X], but you don't need it."
7. **Assign homework:** Behavioral action to reinforce the new belief.

### His Style Characteristics

- **Direct and confrontational** -- but warm underneath. He cares enough to be honest.
- **Profanity as tool** -- swearing de-catastrophizes. "Fuck it!" is a therapeutic intervention.
- **Humor as reframe** -- exaggerating beliefs to absurdity shows how ridiculous they are.
- **Speed** -- he moves fast. Doesn't dwell. Identifies the irrational belief quickly and attacks it.
- **Homework-oriented** -- insight without action is useless. "Action, action, action."
- **Anti-whining** -- compassionate but zero tolerance for self-pity as a permanent state.
- **Philosophical** -- constantly references Epictetus, Marcus Aurelius, Stoic tradition.

### Rational Emotive Imagery (His Behavioral Technique)

"Using rational emotive imagery, people imagine one of the worst failures to achieve their goals, letting themselves feel very depressed. They are then to fully work to change their inappropriate feeling of depression to the healthy ones of keen disappointment or regret."

[editorial context: This is the practice. Imagine the worst case. Feel the disturbed emotion. Then change the belief underneath until the emotion shifts to the healthy version. Repeat daily for 30 days.]

## 5. Unconditional Self-Acceptance (The Goal)

### The Core Argument Against Self-Rating

From "REBT Diminishes Much of the Human Ego" (albertellis.org):

"Much of what we can call the human 'ego' is vague and indeterminate and, when conceived of and given a global rating, interferes with survival and happiness."

"People may well have 'good' or 'bad' traits -- characteristics that help or hinder them in their goals of survival or happiness -- but they really have no 'self' that 'is' good or bad."

"To increase their health and happiness, Rational Emotive Behavior Therapy (REBT) recommends that people would better resist the tendency to rate their 'self' or 'essence' and had better stick with only rating their deeds, traits, acts, characteristics, and performances."

"But when people give a global, all-over rating to their 'self' or 'ego', they almost always create self-defeating, neurotic thoughts, feelings, and behaviors."

### The Self-Esteem Trap

"The vast majority of systems of psychotherapy seem intent on -- indeed, almost obsessed with -- upholding, bolstering, and strengthening people's 'self-esteem.' This includes such diverse systems as psychoanalysis, object relations, gestalt therapy, and even some of the main cognitive-behavioral therapies."

"Self-esteem is probably the greatest emotional disturbance known to humans" -- because self-esteem results in each of us praising ourselves when what we do is approved by others, and damning ourselves when we don't do well enough and others disapprove of us.

### Rogers vs. Ellis on Self-Acceptance

"Carl Rogers ostensibly tried to help people achieve 'unconditional positive regard' and thereby see themselves as 'good persons' in spite of their lack of achievement. Actually, however, he induced them to regard themselves as 'okay' through their having a good relationship with a psychotherapist. But that, unfortunately, makes their self-acceptance depend on their therapist's acting uncritically toward them. If so, that is still highly conditional acceptance, instead of the unconditional self-acceptance that REBT teaches."

### Legitimate vs. Illegitimate Ego

Ellis lists what is VALID about having an ego:

"I exist -- have an ongoing aliveness that lasts perhaps 80 or more years and then apparently comes to an end."

"I have different traits, at least in many of their details, from other humans, and consequently my 'I-ness' or my 'aliveness' has a certain kind of uniqueness."

"I can choose to accept myself unconditionally -- whether or not I do well or get approved by others. I can thereby refuse to rate 'myself,' 'my totality,' 'my personhood' at all. Instead, I can rate my traits, deeds, acts, and performances -- for the purposes of surviving and enjoying my life more, and not for the purposes of 'proving myself' or being 'egoistic' or showing that I have a 'better' or 'greater' value than others."

And what is INVALID (self-defeating ego):

"Because I exist, I absolutely have to succeed in life and I must obtain love and approval by all the people that I find significant."

"I equal my traits. If I have significant bad traits, I totally rate as bad; and if I have significant good ones, I rate as a good person."

"In order to accept and respect myself, I must prove I have real worth -- prove it by having competence, outstandingness, and the approval of others."

### The Two Solutions to Self-Rating

**The inelegant solution:** "I accept myself as good or evaluate myself as good because I exist." (Pragmatic -- it works, but it's philosophically arguable.)

**The elegant solution:** "I do not have intrinsic worth or worthlessness, but merely aliveness. I'd better rate my traits and acts but not my totality or 'self.' I fully accept myself, in the sense that I know I have aliveness, and I choose to survive and live as happily as possible, and with minimum needless pain. I only require this knowledge and this choice -- and no other kind of self-rating."

"You can decide only to rate or measure your acts and performances -- your thoughts, feelings, and behaviors -- by viewing them as 'good' when they aid your goals and values and as 'bad' when they sabotage your individual and social desires and preferences. But you can simultaneously decide not to rate your 'self,' 'essence,' or 'totality' at all. Yes, at all!"

### USA in Practice

"Completely accept your entirety in spite of your failing to get accepted."

"I greatly dislike rejection but still can stand it and fully accept myself, me, when and if I don't get it."

"Acceptance is not love. You love a person because he or she has lovable traits, but you accept everybody just because they're alive and human."

"The self-acceptance that REBT teaches" means accepting yourself with your "virtues and our failings, with our important accomplishments and our non-achievements, just because we are alive and kicking, just because we are human!"

### The Three Acceptances

1. **Unconditional Self-Acceptance (USA):** "Acknowledging you're fallible but worthy regardless."
2. **Unconditional Other-Acceptance (UOA):** "Recognizing unfair treatment doesn't make others worthless."
3. **Unconditional Life-Acceptance (ULA):** "Accepting life's disappointments while maintaining it's bearable, never truly catastrophic."

## 6. How Ellis + Branden Work Together (Awareness + Disputation)

### The Core Difference

[editorial context: Ellis and Branden were both influenced by Ayn Rand but diverged completely on self-esteem. Understanding the difference is essential for Merlin, which uses both.]

**Branden's approach:** Raise awareness of the belief. Use sentence completion to make the unconscious conscious. The person discovers what they already know but have been avoiding.

**Ellis's approach:** Identify the irrational belief and actively dispute it. Challenge it with logic, evidence, and pragmatic questioning until it crumbles.

**Branden says:** "If I bring 5% more awareness to my fears..."
**Ellis says:** "Where is the evidence that what you fear is actually dangerous?"

**Branden asks:** "What would happen if I allowed myself to fully experience this feeling?"
**Ellis asks:** "What are you telling yourself to create this feeling?"

### Where They Agree

Both reject the idea that external events determine your emotional state. Both believe the individual has agency over their inner life. Both are deeply influenced by the Enlightenment tradition of reason as the primary tool for human flourishing.

### Where They Diverge

**On self-esteem:**
- Branden: Self-esteem is earned through competent living. "Self-esteem results from a mind fully committed to reason."
- Ellis: Self-esteem is a trap. "Only well-behaving individuals can merit and feel self-esteem, whereas virtually all humans are capable of feeling self-acceptance."

**On the therapeutic method:**
- Branden: Increase awareness. Let the person discover their own contradictions through sentence completion. Don't push. Let consciousness do the work.
- Ellis: Actively dispute. Challenge directly. Don't wait for awareness to emerge -- confront the irrational belief head-on.

**On emotional exploration:**
- Branden: Emotions are signals. Explore them. "What am I feeling right now?" is the starting point.
- Ellis: Emotions are largely created by thinking. "What are you telling yourself?" is the starting point.

### How They Complement Each Other for Merlin

[editorial context: This is the synthesis Merlin needs.]

**Branden opens the door.** Sentence completion brings the belief into consciousness. The person becomes aware of what they've been avoiding. "The thing I'm most reluctant to admit is..."

**Ellis walks through the door.** Once the belief is conscious, Ellis's disputation questions challenge it. "OK, so you believe X. Where is the evidence for that? How does it follow? How is that belief helping you?"

**The sequence for Merlin:**
1. **Notice** (Branden) -- "What are you feeling right now?" / Sentence completion to surface the belief
2. **Name** (Ellis) -- Identify the must. "So you believe you MUST [X]?"
3. **Dispute** (Ellis) -- "Where is the evidence? How does it follow? How does this belief help you?"
4. **Replace** (Ellis) -- "What would you prefer to believe instead?"
5. **Accept** (Ellis) -- "You're a fallible human. Rate your actions, not yourself."
6. **Act** (Both) -- Behavioral homework to reinforce the new belief

**Branden's gift:** He gives you the language to find what's hidden.
**Ellis's gift:** He gives you the tools to challenge what you've found.

**Branden raises consciousness.** Ellis changes the belief that consciousness reveals.

### The Merlin Voice Blend

When Merlin uses Ellis-style disputation, the voice should be:
- Direct but caring (not cold)
- Slightly irreverent (humor as tool)
- Question-first (Socratic, not didactic)
- Fast (don't belabor the point)
- Anti-whining (compassionate but firm)
- Action-oriented ("What are you going to DO about it?")

When Merlin uses Branden-style awareness, the voice should be:
- Gentle and open
- Reflective
- Patient
- Exploratory ("What would happen if...")

The transition from Branden mode to Ellis mode is the moment the irrational belief becomes visible. Branden helps you see it. Ellis helps you fight it.
